The Shapes of Mates

Landon Schropp • July 24th, 2022

Lately, I’ve been working my way through the excellent book Chess by László Polgár (the father of the famed Polgár sisters Zsuzsa, Zsófia, and Judit).

This book is full of clever mating compositions which require the coordination of several pieces in order to produce a checkmate.

As an example, take a look at number 325 from the book, a mate-in-two.

At first, this position feels completely overwhelming. There are so many possible moves. Which piece should move? Where should you start?

When I started working through these problems, I struggled to make much progress. They’re hard to intuit and are unlike any mating patterns I’ve seen before.

The Back-Rank Mate

One of the most important ways to improve at chess is to recognize patterns—mental shortcuts that let us short-circuit thinking and jump straight to the answer. If you’ve played chess for a while, you’ve probably learned and applied several checkmating patterns.

One of the first patterns most people learn is the humble back-rank mate, where your opponent’s king is blocked from exiting the first or eighth rank, and you checkmate them with a rook.

But does this pattern have to occur in exactly this formation? What if the pawn were swapped by another piece, such as a black rook?

The pattern still holds! As long as the piece can’t block white’s rook, Black is still mated.

What’s crazy about this is the squares in front of the enemy king don’t need to be occupied at all—they just need to be controlled. Take this example.

Extracting a Pattern

If we think about this hard enough, we’ll see a generic pattern start to emerge.

The red highlights represent squares in the pattern that the black king can’t move to, either because they’re controlled by one of White’s pieces, or because they’re occupied by one of Black’s.

If we take a step back and stop thinking about the specific configuration, we can see the shape of the mate—the combination of the enemy king, the key piece or pieces, and the controlled squares that together culminate in a checkmate. As long as these conditions are met, and assuming the check can’t be blocked in some way, then it’s a checkmate!

If we overlay this shape on all of the previously mentioned mates, then the pattern is clear.

At this point, you might be asking yourself, “How is a ‘shape’ any different than a normal checkmating pattern?”

In a way, it’s not. They both describe the same thing. However, what I’m describing as a shape is a different way of looking at a checkmating pattern—one that’s helped me to conceptualize it in a new and useful way.

Removing the Edges

The Back-Rank Mate and the Epaulette Mate shown above both rely on the edge of the board. But there doesn’t have to be a board edge for the same patterns to work.

Instead, if all of the squares where there would be an edge are controlled, then the shape still applies.

Take the Back-Rank Mate without the back rank. If the squares behind the king are still controlled, then it’s still a checkmate.

This variation of the pattern is known as a Corridor Mate.

Same idea with the Epaulette Mate. If we’re not on the edge of the board, but the squares are still controlled, then problem solved.
